## Recommendation Summary

This is worth reading today because it tackles a central weakness in current inference-time scaling: fixed sampling budgets waste compute on easy questions and still under-serve hard ones. CaTS trains models to produce better calibrated confidence estimates, then uses those estimates to improve or stop repeated-sampling methods such as Best-of-N, Self-Consistency, and Adaptive Self-Consistency. The validation signal is solid for a recent paper: ICLR 2026 acceptance, experiments across three model families and nine datasets, ablations, comparisons to reward-model scoring, and released code.

## Why This Paper Is Worth Reading Now

Reasoning research is shifting from "more thinking" to "better compute allocation." CaTS is a clean representative of that shift because it asks whether the model can cheaply estimate when additional reasoning samples are useful, without relying on a separate reward model at inference time. Its practical value is highest for systems that already use repeated sampling or self-consistency and need a principled way to cut cost without giving up accuracy.

## Core Idea

- Distill Self-Consistency-derived confidence into the model itself, producing more reliable answer-confidence estimates with one extra confidence query.
- Use calibrated confidence to stop Best-of-N sampling early or weight Self-Consistency votes by estimated answer reliability.
- Show that confidence-guided sampling can preserve or improve reasoning accuracy while using fewer samples across multiple models and reasoning datasets.

## Broader Trend

Adaptive test-time compute for LLM reasoning: moving away from fixed-length chain-of-thought and fixed-N sampling toward confidence, verifier, planner, and budget-controller methods that spend inference compute selectively.

## What To Be Skeptical About

- The method depends on the quality and transferability of calibrated confidence; badly calibrated models can make early stopping confidently wrong.
- The strongest story is for repeated-sampling pipelines, so the gain may be smaller for models or tasks where a single long reasoning trace dominates.
- The evaluation is benchmark-centered; deployment to open-ended agentic reasoning or tool-use workflows still needs stronger evidence.
- OpenReview confirms ICLR 2026 acceptance, but detailed public review text was not visible in the retrieved page, so the review-discussion signal is positive but limited rather than deeply inspectable.
