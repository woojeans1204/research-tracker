## Paper

**What If We Allocate Test-Time Compute Adaptively?**  
Ahsan Bilal, Ahmed Mohsin, Muhammad Umer, Ali Subhan, Hassan Rizwan, Ayesha Mohsin, Dean Hougen  
- arXiv: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.01070
- Submitted: 2026-02-01
- Latest version checked: v4, 2026-04-06
- Venue signal: arXiv listing/search results report ICML 2026 regular/acceptance signals; final proceedings page not verified in this run.

## Recommendation Summary

This is a good paper to read now because it treats LLM reasoning inference as an allocation problem, not just a "think longer" or "sample more" problem. The proposed framework uses a process reward model to guide pruning, expansion, and final trajectory selection during test-time reasoning. The paper is especially relevant to current work on adaptive test-time compute, verifier-guided reasoning, and compute-efficient reasoning systems.

## Why This Paper Is Worth Reading Now

Recent reasoning work is moving from brute-force test-time scaling toward systems that decide where extra inference budget should go. This paper sits squarely in that transition: it asks whether compute should be allocated dynamically based on problem difficulty and intermediate reasoning quality. Its validation signals are promising for a recent paper: hard math benchmarks, PRM-guided trajectory control, compute-efficiency metrics, and ablation analysis.

## Core Idea

- Treat reasoning as iterative trajectory generation and selection rather than one-shot CoT or fixed Best-of-N.
- Use a process reward model as a unified control signal for step-level pruning, expansion, and final answer selection.
- Allocate compute dynamically across planning, tool choice, exploration, and verification instead of applying the same budget to every problem.

## Broader Trend

Adaptive test-time compute for LLM reasoning: moving from longer chains and more samples toward controller/verifier systems that route inference effort based on uncertainty, difficulty, and intermediate reasoning quality.

## What To Be Skeptical About

- PRM quality is central; weak verifiers could make adaptive allocation confidently wrong.
- The evaluation is math-heavy, so transfer to open-ended reasoning, agents, or messy tool-use tasks is not guaranteed.
- Controller and verifier overhead matter; the compute-efficiency story depends on whether full system cost is counted fairly.
- Citation signals are still immature, so this should be read as a strong recent candidate rather than a settled durable classic.

## Additional Recommendation - Plan and Budget

**Plan and Budget: Effective and Efficient Test-Time Scaling on Reasoning Large Language Models**  
Junhong Lin, Xinyue Zeng, Jie Zhu, Song Wang, Julian Shun, Jun Wu, Dawei Zhou  
- OpenReview: https://openreview.net/forum?id=ctspw4CqbS
- arXiv: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.16122

- Latest arXiv revision checked: v3, 2026-03-02
- Domain: LLM reasoning
- Priority: high
- Topic tags: reasoning, inference-time-scaling, test-time-compute, planning, efficiency

### Recommendation Summary

This is especially worth reading today because it reframes test-time reasoning as structured compute allocation rather than simply longer chain-of-thought or larger Best-of-N sampling. The paper has stronger validation signals than most recent budget-allocation preprints: ICLR 2026 acceptance, a theoretical budget-allocation model, experiments across tasks and models, reported accuracy and token-efficiency gains, and released code.

### Why This Paper Is Worth Reading Now

The reasoning field is converging on a practical question: how should systems spend inference tokens when reasoning models can overthink easy items and underthink hard ones? Plan-and-Budget is a durable reference point for that shift because it connects decomposition, uncertainty-aware budget scheduling, and cost-aware evaluation in one model-agnostic framework.

### Core Idea

- Decompose a reasoning problem into sub-questions instead of asking the model to spend one undifferentiated reasoning budget.
- Estimate sub-question difficulty or uncertainty, then allocate more tokens where extra reasoning is likely to pay off.
- Evaluate reasoning with an efficiency-aware metric that balances correctness against token cost, not accuracy alone.

### Broader Trend

Adaptive and controllable test-time compute: moving from brute-force reasoning length toward planners, budget schedulers, verifiers, and stopping rules that make inference-time scaling cheaper and more targeted.

### What To Be Skeptical About

- The decomposition step may be doing a lot of hidden work; failures in planning quality could undermine the budget policy.
- Reported gains should be read with full cost accounting, including planning overhead and any extra prompting passes.
- Math-style and benchmark-style reasoning may not predict transfer to open-ended agentic workflows or knowledge-heavy tasks.
- The arXiv preprint predates the six-month window, although the ICLR 2026 publication and latest revision fall inside it.
