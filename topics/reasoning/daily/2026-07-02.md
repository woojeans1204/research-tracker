### Recommendation summary
This is the reasoning paper I would read today because it asks a durable question: under weak rewards, when does RLVR actually teach a model to reason instead of fitting the training signal? The paper is especially useful because it studies scarce data, noisy rewards, and self-supervised proxy rewards across model families and reasoning domains, then ties generalization to reward-saturation dynamics and pre-RL reasoning faithfulness.

### Why it matters now
As reasoning models get stronger, clean verifiable supervision becomes harder and more expensive to construct. This paper gives a practical diagnostic frame for weak-supervision RLVR: watch whether reward saturation is delayed and whether intermediate reasoning is faithful before RL, rather than assuming more RL or more proxy reward data will generalize.

### Core idea
- RLVR generalization under weak supervision depends on training reward saturation dynamics: prolonged pre-saturation tends to coincide with downstream gains, while rapid saturation often indicates memorization.
- Pre-RL reasoning faithfulness predicts whether weak supervision will generalize better than simple output diversity does.
- SFT on explicit reasoning traces appears necessary for weak-supervision generalization, while domain continual pretraining can amplify that effect.

### Validation signal
The paper is an April 2026 arXiv preprint with a project page, public code, and released models. I did not find a top-conference acceptance or OpenReview page, so the external review signal is currently limited; the main strength is the systematic empirical design rather than venue validation.

### What to read carefully
- Check whether the saturation-dynamics diagnostic remains predictive beyond the tested Qwen/Llama families and math, science, and graph reasoning domains.
- Treat the SFT-plus-continual-pretraining conclusion as suggestive but implementation-sensitive, since the exact trace format and data mixture may matter a lot.
- Look closely at proxy-reward settings: majority vote and self-certainty are useful stress tests, but they may still be easier than truly underspecified real-world reasoning feedback.
